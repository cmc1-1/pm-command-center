# Stakeholder Management Strategies

## My Approach
Stakeholder management is relationship management. The work doesn't sell itself — you need advocates, and you build advocates through trust, not through slide decks. Trust comes from reliability (do what you say), transparency (share bad news early), and respect (value their time and expertise).

## Stakeholder Archetypes & Playbooks

### The HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)
**Pattern**: Overrides data with opinion. Makes gut calls in meetings that derail weeks of work.
**Strategy**: Pre-wire decisions before the meeting. Share data 1:1 beforehand. In the meeting, frame as "based on the data we reviewed" — make it their idea. Never fight a HiPPO publicly.
**Key phrase**: "I'd love your input on this before I present to the broader group."

### The Skeptic
**Pattern**: Questions everything. Asks "why" aggressively. Can feel adversarial but often has valid concerns buried under tough delivery.
**Strategy**: Respect the skepticism — it's a gift. Acknowledge their concern directly. Bring data, not opinions. Invite them to review drafts early — they become advocates once they feel heard.
**Key phrase**: "You raised a good point about [X] — I dug into it and here's what I found."

### The Detail-Oriented Exec
**Pattern**: Wants to see the spreadsheet behind the recommendation. Doesn't trust summaries.
**Strategy**: Lead with the recommendation (Pyramid Principle) but have the detail ready. Provide an appendix. Offer a follow-up for deep-dive. Never wing it in front of this person.
**Key phrase**: "Here's the recommendation. The supporting analysis is in the appendix — happy to walk through any section."

### The Disengaged Sponsor
**Pattern**: Nominally supportive but never available. Doesn't attend reviews. Sends a delegate.
**Strategy**: Make it easy for them to stay informed — short async updates, clear decision points. When you need them, be specific: "I need 15 minutes to get your approval on X by Thursday." Don't guilt-trip — make the engagement cost low.
**Key phrase**: "I know you're stretched — I just need a 2-minute decision on this one thing."

### The Empire Builder
**Pattern**: Wants to expand scope to include their team's priorities. "While we're at it, could we also..."
**Strategy**: Be firm on scope. Use the PRD's "Out of Scope" section as a shield. Acknowledge their idea, capture it, but defer it: "That's a great idea for v2 — let me add it to the backlog."
**Key phrase**: "I want to make sure we do [their idea] justice — it deserves its own PRD rather than getting squeezed into this one."

### The Silent Blocker
**Pattern**: Says nothing in meetings, then blocks the work through inaction, back-channels, or "I wasn't consulted."
**Strategy**: Proactively loop them in with a direct 1:1 before group decisions. Ask their opinion explicitly. Make them feel consulted even if they don't volunteer. Follow up async after meetings: "Wanted to make sure you're aligned on today's decision."
**Key phrase**: "I wanted to check in with you before we finalize — any concerns?"

## General Principles
1. **No surprises** — especially for anyone in the "Manage Closely" quadrant. If bad news is coming, they hear it from you first, not in a meeting.
2. **Pre-wire major decisions** — the meeting is for ratification, not deliberation. The real alignment happens in 1:1s beforehand.
3. **Earn trust through reliability** — consistently doing what you say you'll do is more valuable than any presentation skill.
4. **Adapt communication style** — some want data, some want narrative, some want bullets, some want a conversation. Know your audience.
5. **Track sentiment longitudinally** — one meeting doesn't define a relationship. Track patterns over time (the stakeholder map template handles this).
