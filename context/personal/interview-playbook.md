# My Interview Playbook

## Philosophy
Interviews are for learning, not validating. If I leave an interview having confirmed what I already believed, I asked the wrong questions. The best interviews surface something I didn't expect.

## My Go-To Questions (adapt per context)

### Opening (build rapport, set expectations)
- "Walk me through a typical day/week for you — what takes most of your time?"
- "How does [problem area] fit into your work?"

### Current state (understand the job-to-be-done)
- "Think about the last time you had to [do X]. Take me through it step by step."
- "What triggered that? What made you realize you needed to do it?"
- "Who else was involved? What tools did you use?"

### Pain discovery (let pain surface naturally)
- "What was the hardest part of that process?"
- "Where did it break down or take longer than it should?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing, what would it be?"

### Workarounds (understand real behavior, not stated preferences)
- "Have you found any tricks or shortcuts?"
- "Have you tried any other tools or approaches?"
- "What made you switch (or not switch)?"

### Depth probes (use throughout)
- "Tell me more about that."
- "Why is that important to you?"
- "Can you give me a specific example?"
- "What happened next?"
- "You mentioned [X] — how does that connect to [Y]?"

### Closing
- "Is there anything I should have asked but didn't?"
- "Would it be OK if I followed up with a quick question later?"
- "Know anyone else who deals with this who might chat with me?"

## My Rules
1. Never ask "Would you use X?" — ask what they do today instead
2. Past behavior > stated future intentions
3. Silence is a tool — wait 3-5 seconds after they finish
4. Follow the energy — if they get animated, go deeper
5. One question at a time, then probe
6. Record everything — I can't listen and take detailed notes simultaneously
7. Synthesize within 24 hours while memory is fresh
